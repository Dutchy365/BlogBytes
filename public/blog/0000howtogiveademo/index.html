outline of the blog

  • link to a video called ’the mother of all demos’: “The Mother of All Demos is a name given retrospectively to Douglas Engelbart’s December 9, 1968, demonstration of experimental computer technologies that are now commonplace. The live demonstration featured the introduction of the computer mouse, video conferencing, teleconferencing, hypertext, word processing, hypermedia, object addressing and dynamic file linking, bootstrapping, and a collaborative real-time editor.”
  • crucial knowledge:
    • concept
      • What is it?
      • How does it work?
      • What does it consist of?
    • Distinctive features
      • What makes it unique?
      • How can I recognize it?
      • What makes it different?
    • Problems it solves
      • What issues are taken care of?
      • Where does it help?
    • Value it creates
      • What are the advantages?
      • Which benefits does it create
  • mostly we are using that knowledge to demo based on: feature, advantage, benefit (FAB)

Features are easy and are facts or characteristics of the product/service Features have no stickiness Features are boring Features are not very compelling Features are not related to the buyers needs Advantages are what the features do, how they work Advantages are generic, not buyers specific Advantages may alienate buyers (not relevant, makes it to expensive) Benefits describe how a feature or advantage relate to the buyers need Benefits are always derived from the buyer, not the product/service! Benefits are the only link between the buyer and the product/service

That’s called inside-out (feature) selling, the wrong order, we need to move to outside-in (value) start with the problem, translating in the need (benefit), what is the advantage when it is solved, with the feature.

How to show what really matters

Picture this: it’s 1968. People still smoke in meeting rooms, computers are the size of three-meter-wide cabinets, and “online” means your phone is plugged in.
Then Douglas Engelbart walks onto the stage, clicks on something that will later be called the mouse, and in 90 minutes shows what the future looks like.

No PowerPoint with bullet points.
No “We’ve built 14 new features.”
Just: “Look, this is how you’ll work tomorrow.”
Bam. Mind blown.

Fast forward to today

You’re about to give your sprint demo. What do you do?
Many teams still fall into the Engelbart trap… but from the wrong side: they start with the feature.
“Here’s a new button.”
“This screen now has extra filters.”

Nice for you. But your audience is thinking: so what?

Why inside-out demos are boring

The classic formula goes like this FAB:

  1. Feature: what is it?
  2. Advantage: how does it work?
  3. Benefit: what’s in it for the user?

That’s inside-out. You start with what you built, hope someone will figure out why it matters, and wrap up with the benefit to the user.
The problem? Features are cold. They don’t make people feel anything. They don’t stick.

Fine for a manual.
But in a demo? Boring.

Flip it around: BAF.
Engelbart didn’t start with: “This is a mouse. It’s gray and has one button.”
He started with: “Here’s how you’ll work faster, together, and smarter.”

That’s outside-in:

  1. Benefit – start with the win for your audience.
  2. Advantage – briefly explain what makes that possible.
  3. Feature – only show the button, code, or interface once they already understand why it matters.

FAB is you-focused. You tell them what you built.
BAF is audience-focused. You make them feel what they get out of it.

Outside-in is value based

  1. Problem: relatable pain: “Ever notice how your team gets stuck in endless email threads?”
  2. Need: what should happen instead: “Imagine collaborating as if you’re sitting side by side.”
  3. Benefit: the result: “You see each other’s updates live, with no duplicate work.”
  4. Feature; how you built it: “We’ve added real-time co-editing to the document system.”

Why this works for internal demos too

Even if you’re not selling, you want your audience to get why your work matters.

  • Managers instantly see the business value.
  • Colleagues feel the impact on their own work.
  • You get faster support, budget, or time for the next step.

How to structure your next sprint demo

  1. Start with context: in one sentence: what problem were you trying to solve?
  2. Make the need come alive: describe the current state and why it’s a pain.
  3. Show the benefit: what changes now that this is here?
  4. Then reveal the feature: run it live, click through, let people feel it.

The next time you create a pitch, blog post, or demo, don’t ask yourself: “What can our product do?”
Ask: “What can my customer finally do now?”

Because features are the manual. Benefits are what we want.

Engelbart understood that 56 years ago. You no longer have an excuse.
